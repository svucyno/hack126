Skin Tone → Colour Palette Mapping
Translates the user's skin tone into a personalised colour guide
What this step does
Every user selects their skin tone using the Monk Scale — a 7-point scale from very fair (1) to very deep (7). The
engine immediately translates that number into three lists of colours that will guide every decision in the steps that
follow:
Flattering Colours
Colours scientifically known to complement that skin tone — the engine will actively try to
include these in the final outfit.
Neutral Anchors
Safe, versatile colours (whites, beiges, greys, blacks) that work with any skin tone and help
balance the outfit visually.
Colours to Avoid
Shades that tend to wash out or clash with that specific skin tone — the engine filters these
out before building any combinations.

2
Colour Harmony Filter
Removes wardrobe items whose colours clash with the user's skin tone or each other
What this step does
Before any outfit can be assembled, every item in the user's wardrobe is tested against two standards — does its
colour flatter the skin tone, and will it harmonise with other items in an outfit? Items that fail both tests are removed
from consideration for this session.
The Three Harmony Rules
Rule
What It Means
Real-World Example
Complementary Colours
Colours sitting opposite each other on the
colour wheel. They create a bold,
high-contrast look that feels intentional and
polished.
A cobalt blue top paired with a burnt orange
bottom — the contrast makes each colour pop
more vividly.
Analogous Colours
Colours sitting next to each other on the
colour wheel. They create a calm, cohesive
look where everything flows together
naturally.
An olive green top, mustard yellow bottom, and
rust-toned accessories — all warm, earthy tones
that feel curated without trying too hard.
Neutral Anchor Rule
Every outfit must include at least one neutral
— white, black, beige, grey, or camel.
Neutrals prevent the outfit from becoming
overwhelming, letting bolder pieces breathe.
A bold fuchsia top works beautifully with white
trousers and black shoes. Without that neutral,
adding a printed bottom would create visual
chaos.
How an Item Passes or Fails the Filter
■ Direct skin match
The item's colour is in the user's personal flattering palette from Step 1. This is the best
possible result — the item is proven to suit this skin tone.
■ Harmonises with skin
palette
The item's colour isn't in the palette directly, but it sits in a complementary or analogous
relationship to a colour that is. It still flatters.
■ Is a neutral
White, black, beige, grey, camel — always allowed through because they anchor the outfit
and complement every skin tone.
■ No harmony found
The item's colour doesn't flatter the skin tone, doesn't harmonise with the palette, and isn't a
neutral. It is removed from this session's wardrobe.
Why this matters
Without this filter, the combination builder in Step 3 would assemble thousands of outfits — including ones where a
mustard-yellow skirt is paired with a lilac top for someone with a warm olive skin tone. This filter ensures every item that
enters Step 3 has already earned its place.

3
Combination Builder
Assembles every possible outfit from the filtered wardrobe
What this step does
Every outfit needs four things — a top, a bottom, shoes, and an accessory. This step takes the filtered wardrobe from
Step 2 and systematically assembles every valid four-piece combination. If the wardrobe is missing items in any
category, the engine fills that gap using a curated sample dataset of items already proven to suit the user's skin tone
and occasion.
The Four Outfit Slots
Top
T-shirt, shirt, blouse, kurta, jacket, sweatshirt
Bottom
Jeans, trousers, skirt, shorts, churidar, palazzos
Shoes
Sneakers, heels, flats, loafers, juttis, boots
Accessory
Watch, bag, belt, scarf, dupatta, jewellery
Gap Filling — When the Wardrobe Is Incomplete
How gap filling works
If the user hasn't uploaded any shoes, for example, the engine doesn't give up. It pulls from a pre-curated sample dataset
of shoes, filters them to only include options that suit the user's skin tone and match the selected occasion, and uses those
to complete the outfit. The user sees the gap clearly marked in the output so they know those shoes came from the sample,
not their wardrobe.
How Combinations Are Counted
The engine tries every possible combination of items across all four slots. For example — if the filtered wardrobe has 2
tops, 2 bottoms, 2 pairs of shoes and 1 accessory, that produces 2 × 2 × 2 × 1 = 8 combinations. Each combination is
then checked to confirm all four items are tagged for the chosen occasion before passing forward to Step 4.
The Anchor Feature — Building an Outfit Around an Uploaded Item
Module 1 allows users to upload a photo of a specific clothing item they want to wear. This item becomes the anchor
— the outfit is built around it rather than freely assembled. The anchor's slot is locked, and the remaining three slots
are chosen to complement it specifically.
1 — Photo is uploaded
The user uploads a photo of the item they want to build around — for example, a
green embroidered kurta.
2 — Colour is extracted
ColourThief automatically detects the dominant colour in the photo and converts it to
a precise colour code. No manual input needed.
3 — Claude Vision analyses the
item
The AI examines the photo and identifies: what type of clothing it is (top, bottom,
shoes, or accessory), its pattern (solid, printed, embroidered), how formal it is on a
scale of 1–5, and which occasions it suits.

4
Style Layer — Persona Classification
Identifies the style vibe of every assembled outfit combination
What this step does
Once all valid combinations have been assembled, each outfit is stamped with a style persona — a label that captures
the overall aesthetic and character of the look. This helps users understand not just what to wear, but the story the
outfit tells. It also powers the 'Style Persona' card shown in the output.
The Six Style Personas
■ Ethnic Traditional
Rooted in cultural heritage. Typically includes kurtas, dupattas, churidars, palazzos, and
juttis. Rich embroidery and block prints. Occasions range from festive gatherings to formal
ethnic events.
■ Minimalist
Clean lines, quiet palette, no unnecessary detail. Solid colours dominate, accessories are
simple and functional. The look is deliberate — less is genuinely more.
■ Casual Chic
Relaxed but intentional. Elevated basics — a well-cut blouse, clean jeans, simple flats. The
kind of outfit that looks effortless but clearly has thought behind it.
■ Boho
Earthy tones, flowing silhouettes, layered textures. Printed and tie-dye patterns, natural
materials. Has a free-spirited, organic quality.
■ Streetwear
Bold, urban, graphic-driven. Hoodies, joggers, statement sneakers, caps. Confidence is the
primary accessory.
■ Smart Formal
Structured and polished. Blazers, tailored trousers, formal shirts, dress shoes.
Occasion-appropriate and professionally assembled.
How a Persona Is Decided — The Four Signals
Item Types (35% weight)
The most important signal. Which specific clothing types appear in the combo? A
kurta immediately signals Ethnic Traditional. A hoodie signals Streetwear. The
engine checks every item against each persona's expected item list.
Pattern Composition (25% weight)
What patterns are present across the four items? Solid colours lean Minimalist or
Smart Formal. Embroidery and block prints lean Ethnic Traditional. Graphics lean
Streetwear. Tie-dye leans Boho.
Formality Average (25% weight)
Each item is scored 1 (very casual) to 5 (very formal). The average across all four
items is compared against each persona's expected formality range. A Minimalist
outfit typically averages 2–4, while Streetwear averages 1–2.
Style Tag Overlap (15% weight)
When Claude Vision analyses an uploaded photo, it assigns style tags to the item.
These tags are compared with each persona's expected tags to give the
classification an extra nudge in the right direction.
Example classification
An outfit of embroidered kurta + churidar + juttis + dupatta scores very highly on item types (all four are ethnic-wear
subtypes), highly on patterns (embroidery is a strong ethnic signal), within the correct formality range (2–4), and matches
ethnic_traditional style tags. It receives a confidence score of 0.87 and is stamped ■ Ethnic Traditional.

Outfit Balancer
Rejects combinations that are visually overwhelming or stylistically inconsistent
What this step does
Not every mathematically valid combination makes a good outfit. This step acts as a quality gate, checking two
specific problems that colour harmony alone cannot catch — outfits that are too visually busy, and outfits where the
items don't belong in the same world of formality.
Rule 1 — Visual Weight Check
Every clothing item carries a visual weight — a measure of how much attention it demands from the eye. A plain white
shirt demands very little. An embroidered, bright-red printed kurta demands a lot. The rule is simple:
Maximum 1 bold item per outfit.
One statement piece is intentional style. Two statement pieces compete for attention. Three is visual noise. Outfits with more than
one bold item are rejected.

6
Score & Rank
Every surviving outfit is scored out of 100 — best outfit + 2 alternatives returned
What this step does
Every outfit that passed the Balancer's quality gate is now scored out of 100 across three dimensions. The scores are
combined, all outfits are ranked highest to lowest, and the top three are returned — one best recommendation and two
alternatives the user can swipe between.
Colour Harmony
40 pts
Tests how well the four items' colours work together as a complete colour
story. Every pair of items is tested — complementary pairs score highest (10
pts), analogous pairs score well (7 pts), neutral anchors score reliably (8 pts).
Weak pairings score only 2 pts. The total is normalised to 40 points.
Skin Tone Match
30 pts
Tests how well each item individually flatters the user's specific skin tone.
Items closer to the face (top, accessory) are weighted more heavily than
items further away (bottom, shoes) — because what sits near your face has
the biggest impact on how a colour reads against your skin. Direct palette
match = 10 pts, harmony match = 7 pts, neutral = 5 pts.
Occasion Fit
30 pts
Tests whether each item is actually appropriate for the selected occasion.
Both checks must pass for full points — the item must be tagged for that
occasion, and its formality level must fall within the expected range for that
occasion. Failing both checks earns only 1 point out of 8.

Executive Summary
DripFit is an AI-powered outfit suggestion platform that helps individuals build complete, colour-harmonious outfits
tailored to their personal wardrobe, skin tone, and occasion. The product solves a universal daily frustration —
standing in front of a full wardrobe and not knowing what to wear — through a combination of scientific colour theory
and AI-powered image analysis.
Unlike existing platforms such as Myntra, Stitch Fix, and ASOS — which focus almost exclusively on selling new items
— DripFit helps users make the most of what they already own. The result is a product with strong differentiation, low
churn risk, and a clear path to a B2C subscription revenue model.
Product Type
B2C Web Application (Mobile-Responsive)
Core Technology
Django backend + React frontend + Claude Vision API
Business Model
Freemium — Free tier with 3 saved looks / Premium subscription
Target Market
Fashion-conscious individuals aged 18–40 who want to style smarter
MVP Scope
Three modules: Outfit Suggester, Style Me, Fashion Analyser
Key Differentiator
Skin-tone aware colour engine + styles existing wardrobe — not sell new
clothes

Problem Statement
Despite owning full wardrobes, most people struggle daily with the question of what to wear. This is not a trivial
inconvenience — it causes measurable decision fatigue, leads to underused clothing, and results in low confidence
about personal style. Three underlying causes drive this problem:
Lack of colour theory
knowledge
Most people do not know which colours complement their specific skin tone, which
colour combinations are harmonious, or what the rules of visual balance actually
are. This knowledge gap makes outfit building feel like guesswork.
No occasion-aware guidance
Even people who understand basic colour matching often struggle to adapt their
wardrobe to specific occasions — distinguishing what is appropriately casual vs
smart casual vs formal is non-obvious without training.
Existing tools focus on selling,
not styling
Platforms like Myntra, Stitch Fix, and ASOS are fundamentally discovery and
purchase tools. They are commercially incentivised to show users new items to
buy, not to maximise the value of what the user already owns. No mainstream tool
fills this gap.
The Market Gap
No widely accessible tool combines a user's existing wardrobe with skin-tone analysis and colour-harmony rules to deliver
complete, occasion-appropriate outfit suggestions. DripFit fills this gap with a scientifically grounded, AI-augmented
solution.

Three Core Modules — Detailed Behaviour
Module 1 — Outfit
Suggester
Build a complete outfit from your own wardrobe
The primary module. The user uploads their wardrobe items, selects their skin tone and the occasion they are
dressing for, and receives a fully scored, explained outfit recommendation built exclusively from what they own — or
supplemented by the sample dataset where their wardrobe has gaps.
Step
What Happens
1. Sign in
User authenticates via Google OAuth. Subscription tier is loaded.
2. Build wardrobe
User uploads clothing item photos. ColourThief extracts colour. Claude Vision identifies
metadata. Items stored in database.
3. Select skin tone
User picks Monk Scale 1–7 from swatch selector, or optionally uploads a photo for
auto-detection.
4. Select occasion
User picks one occasion from: casual, formal, party, sport, ethnic & festive.
5. Optional anchor
upload
User uploads a photo of one specific item to build the outfit around. That item's slot is locked.
6. Engine runs
All six engine steps execute: palette → harmony filter → combo builder → style layer →
balancer → score & rank.
7. View results
Best outfit + 2 alternatives displayed on 2D figure. Score card, colour card, persona, and
balance summary shown.
8. Save look
User can save the outfit with a personal note. Limited to 3 on free tier.
Outputs: Best outfit on 2D figure · 100-point score breakdown · Colour harmony card · Style persona + rationale · Balance summary ·
2 swipeable alternatives

Get outfit ideas without uploading a wardrobe
A lighter entry point for new users or anyone who wants style inspiration without the commitment of uploading their
wardrobe. The user provides only their skin tone and occasion — the engine draws entirely from the curated sample
dataset to build outfit suggestions. All output cards are identical to Module 1.
Step
What Happens

User picks Monk Scale 1–7 from swatch selector.
2. Select occasion

3. Engine runs

4. View results
Best outfit + 2 alternatives shown on 2D figure with full output cards.
5. Save look
